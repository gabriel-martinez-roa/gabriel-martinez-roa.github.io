Bayesian Persuasion with Signal Uncertainty (JMP)

Gabriel Martinez-Roa

Abstract

The ability to persuade someone is constrained by their understanding of the information they are presented with. I will extend the classic model of Bayesian persuasion (Kamenica and Gentzkow, 2011), to allow for signal uncertainty, and study how this affects the incentives to provide information, and the scope for successfully convincing a decision maker. In the basic model, the commitment to reveal the results of an experiment implies that recommendations are always followed in equilibrium. I show that if the sender cannot commit to reveal some of his private information, recommendations need not be followed. This holds even if the private information is irrelevant for the receiver’s payoff. The partial commitment creates uncertainty about the information structure itself, and this can completely eliminate the possibility of persuasion in equilibrium. I show that whenever biased information is inevitable, news platforms have incentives to exert editorial power and limit, though not eliminate, the frequency of opinions that try to confuse the audience. Similarly, presenting a receiver with opposed views without requiring to disclose their bias, is less informative than having a source with a known biased perspective.
